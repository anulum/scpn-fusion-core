Run batched Kuramoto on a persistent barrier-synchronised worker pool

The batched run still forked and joined rayon once for the fill and once
for the update on every one of its `n_steps` iterations. At the benchmark
size (10 000 oscillators, 500 steps) that is a thousand fork-joins, and
their cost had come to dominate: the multi-thread batched run barely beat
the single-call step kernel, and the per-step work is too small for a
fresh fork-join to amortise.

Enter the worker pool exactly once instead. `kuramoto_run_parallel` gives
each worker a contiguous oscillator slice (`chunks_mut`, disjoint) for the
whole trajectory via `std::thread::scope`, and the steps advance inside the
persistent workers. Per step each worker publishes its partial order
parameter through lock-free atomics, meets the others at a `Barrier`, then
every worker reduces the identical partial set in the same order — so each
derives bit-identical `(R, ψ_r)` with no coordinator — records the history
on worker 0, and updates its own slice in place. A second barrier orders
every worker's partial reads before the next step overwrites them; the
barrier's happens-before makes the `Relaxed` atomics sufficient, and the
disjoint slices mean no update aliases another worker's data. Small
populations (and a single-thread pool) keep the serial path.

Correctness: the element update is reduction-free and every worker reduces
the partials in the same order, so the pool reproduces the serial
trajectory within the parity gate. A new test asserts
`kuramoto_run_parallel` matches `kuramoto_run_serial` to <1e-12 across
worker counts 1..7 (including a count that does not divide the population);
the existing N=4096 parity test now exercises the pool, and all 24
fusion-phase unit + 10 phase parity cases pass.

CI baseline this refines (phase-kernels-benchmark, 4-vCPU, numpy<2.0):
kuramoto_run 7.83x (was 3.16x before the M-3 work). This commit's figure is
measured by the same CI artifact.
